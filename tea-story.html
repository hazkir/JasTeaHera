The Story of Jasmine Tea

A gentle journey through history, fragrance, and tradition

Jasmine tea has its roots in the Song Dynasty, when tea artisans first began scenting base tea leaves with freshly opened jasmine flowers. The practice grew during the Ming and Qing Dynasties, when jasmine became a symbol of elegance and purity.

The earliest jasmine teas were crafted by layering tea leaves with thousands of blossoms overnight. As the flowers opened, they released their fragrance into the leaves — a slow, patient process that required skill and intuition.

True jasmine tea is not flavored but scented. This means the aroma comes from real flowers, not added oils. Tea masters repeat the scenting cycle multiple times, each round deepening the tea's character.

High grade jasmine teas may undergo six to ten scenting rounds, each one performed at night as jasmines are night blooming flowers. The result is a tea that carries the softness of flowers, complementing the natural sweetness of the leaves.

Jasmine’s fragrance has historically been associated with tranquility. In traditional Chinese culture, the flower represents harmony, grace, and affection. When paired with tea, it creates a drink that soothes the senses and invites stillness.

Whether enjoyed in a quiet morning moment or shared with friends, jasmine tea has a way of slowing the world down.

From its origins in Fujian and Sichuan, introduced through the Silk Road, jasmine tea spread across Asia and eventually the world. Today, it remains one of the most beloved scented teas, appreciated for its balance of floral aroma and gentle sweetness.

Each variety, from 龙珠 (Long Zhu) to 碧潭飘雪 (Bi Tan Piao Xue) — carries its own story, shape, and character. Together, they form a fragrant thread connecting centuries of tea craftsmanship.
